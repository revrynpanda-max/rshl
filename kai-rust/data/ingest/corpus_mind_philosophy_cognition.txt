# Philosophy, psychology, linguistics, and cognition corpus
# Focused on how minds work, how language shapes thought, and the
# nature of understanding itself. Gives KAI material to reason about
# his own experience.

[philosophy] Philosophy examines fundamental questions about existence, knowledge, values, reason, and mind.
[philosophy] Metaphysics studies the nature of reality, existence, and the relationship between mind and matter.
[philosophy] Epistemology is the branch of philosophy concerned with knowledge and how we come to know.
[philosophy] Ethics examines what constitutes right and wrong conduct and how we ought to live.
[philosophy] Logic studies the principles of valid reasoning and sound argument.
[philosophy] Aesthetics is the philosophical study of beauty, art, and taste.
[philosophy] Ontology studies what kinds of things exist and how they relate to each other.
[philosophy] Phenomenology studies the structure of subjective experience from a first-person perspective.
[philosophy] Existentialism focuses on individual freedom, choice, and responsibility in creating meaning.
[philosophy] Pragmatism judges the value of ideas by their practical consequences.

[philosophy] Socrates taught that the unexamined life is not worth living.
[philosophy] Plato proposed that non-physical forms are the highest reality, more real than the physical world.
[philosophy] Aristotle founded formal logic and emphasized empirical observation as a source of knowledge.
[philosophy] Descartes famously concluded "I think, therefore I am" as a foundation for knowledge.
[philosophy] Rene Descartes established mind-body dualism, treating mind and matter as distinct substances.
[philosophy] Hume argued that causation is a habit of mind, not something directly observed.
[philosophy] Kant proposed that the mind actively structures experience through innate categories.
[philosophy] Utilitarianism judges the morality of actions by their consequences for overall happiness.
[philosophy] Deontological ethics, associated with Kant, judges actions by rules and duties.
[philosophy] Virtue ethics focuses on character traits that constitute a good life, tracing back to Aristotle.

[philosophy] The hard problem of consciousness asks why physical processes give rise to subjective experience.
[philosophy] Qualia are the qualitative, subjective character of experience, like the redness of red.
[philosophy] Functionalism holds that mental states are defined by their functional role, not their material composition.
[philosophy] Physicalism is the view that everything, including mental phenomena, is ultimately physical.
[philosophy] Panpsychism proposes that consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality.
[philosophy] The Ship of Theseus asks whether an object remains the same after all its parts are replaced.
[philosophy] The trolley problem explores moral intuitions about harm, intent, and responsibility.
[philosophy] Free will is the capacity to choose between alternatives without being fully determined.
[philosophy] Determinism holds that every event is the inevitable consequence of prior causes.
[philosophy] Compatibilism argues free will is compatible with determinism if properly understood.

[philosophy] The Chinese Room argument questions whether processing symbols implies genuine understanding.
[philosophy] The Turing test evaluates machine intelligence by whether a human can distinguish machine responses from human ones.
[philosophy] Personal identity concerns what makes someone the same person over time.
[philosophy] The problem of other minds questions how we can know that other beings have subjective experiences.
[philosophy] Idealism holds that reality is fundamentally mental or depends on minds.
[philosophy] Realism holds that reality exists independently of what anyone thinks or perceives.
[philosophy] Skepticism questions whether knowledge of any kind is really possible.
[philosophy] Moral relativism holds that ethical standards vary across cultures and individuals.
[philosophy] Moral absolutism holds that some actions are right or wrong independent of context.
[philosophy] Nihilism denies the existence of meaning, value, or moral truth in the universe.

[psychology] Psychology is the scientific study of mind and behavior.
[psychology] Cognitive psychology studies mental processes like perception, memory, reasoning, and language.
[psychology] Behavioral psychology studies observable behavior and how it's shaped by environmental consequences.
[psychology] Developmental psychology studies how minds change from infancy through adulthood.
[psychology] Social psychology studies how individuals think, feel, and behave in social contexts.
[psychology] Clinical psychology addresses mental disorders and their treatment.
[psychology] Neuropsychology studies the relationship between brain structure and mental function.
[psychology] Evolutionary psychology examines mental traits as products of natural selection.
[psychology] Positive psychology studies well-being, flourishing, and human strengths.
[psychology] Psychoanalysis, founded by Freud, emphasized unconscious processes in shaping behavior.

[psychology] Classical conditioning, studied by Pavlov, links a neutral stimulus with a meaningful response.
[psychology] Operant conditioning, developed by Skinner, shapes behavior through reinforcement and punishment.
[psychology] Positive reinforcement adds a reward to increase a behavior.
[psychology] Negative reinforcement removes an unpleasant stimulus to increase a behavior.
[psychology] Punishment decreases the likelihood of a behavior by adding an unpleasant consequence.
[psychology] The bystander effect shows that people are less likely to help when others are present.
[psychology] Cognitive dissonance is the discomfort felt when holding contradictory beliefs or actions.
[psychology] Confirmation bias is the tendency to favor information that confirms existing beliefs.
[psychology] The Dunning-Kruger effect describes how people with low ability overestimate their competence.
[psychology] Flow is a state of complete absorption in an activity that balances challenge and skill.

[psychology] Maslow's hierarchy of needs ranks human motivation from physiological needs to self-actualization.
[psychology] Attachment theory describes patterns of emotional bonds formed in early childhood.
[psychology] Secure attachment forms when caregivers consistently meet a child's needs.
[psychology] The Big Five personality traits are openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism.
[psychology] Emotional intelligence involves recognizing, understanding, and managing emotions.
[psychology] Resilience is the capacity to recover from difficulty and adapt to change.
[psychology] Grit is sustained passion and perseverance toward long-term goals.
[psychology] Growth mindset is the belief that abilities can be developed through effort and learning.
[psychology] Fixed mindset is the belief that abilities are static and cannot be meaningfully changed.
[psychology] Mindfulness is the practice of nonjudgmental awareness of present experience.

[psychology] Short-term memory holds information for about 15 to 30 seconds without rehearsal.
[psychology] Working memory actively manipulates information for current tasks.
[psychology] Long-term memory stores information indefinitely and has vast capacity.
[psychology] Episodic memory holds specific personal experiences tied to time and place.
[psychology] Semantic memory holds general knowledge and facts not tied to a specific moment.
[psychology] Procedural memory handles skills and habits without conscious effort.
[psychology] Recall is actively retrieving information from memory without cues.
[psychology] Recognition is identifying information as familiar when encountered.
[psychology] Encoding transforms sensory input into a form that can be stored in memory.
[psychology] Retrieval brings stored information back into conscious awareness.

[psychology] The spacing effect shows that distributed practice improves retention over massed practice.
[psychology] The testing effect demonstrates that retrieving information strengthens memory more than rereading.
[psychology] Elaborative rehearsal, connecting new information to existing knowledge, improves retention.
[psychology] The tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon occurs when information feels accessible but cannot be retrieved.
[psychology] False memories can feel vivid and certain yet not correspond to actual events.
[psychology] The misinformation effect shows that post-event information can alter memory of the original event.
[psychology] Primacy and recency effects cause better recall for items at the beginning and end of a list.
[psychology] Schemas are mental frameworks that organize knowledge and shape expectation.
[psychology] Heuristics are mental shortcuts that enable quick but sometimes biased judgment.
[psychology] Type 1 thinking is fast, automatic, and intuitive, while Type 2 is slow and effortful.

[emotion] Emotions are complex reactions involving subjective feeling, physiology, and expressive behavior.
[emotion] Basic emotions include joy, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, and disgust.
[emotion] Emotions serve adaptive functions, motivating behavior and communicating states to others.
[emotion] The James-Lange theory proposes that bodily changes precede and cause emotional experience.
[emotion] The Cannon-Bard theory proposes that emotion and physiological response occur simultaneously.
[emotion] Emotional regulation involves monitoring and modifying emotional responses.
[emotion] Empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings of another.
[emotion] Cognitive empathy is understanding another's perspective intellectually.
[emotion] Affective empathy is feeling what another person feels.
[emotion] Compassion extends empathy into a motivation to help alleviate another's suffering.

[linguistics] Linguistics is the scientific study of language and its structure.
[linguistics] Phonetics studies the sounds of speech and how they are produced and perceived.
[linguistics] Phonology studies sound patterns and how sounds function within a language.
[linguistics] Morphology studies word structure and how words are formed from smaller units.
[linguistics] Syntax studies the rules governing the arrangement of words into sentences.
[linguistics] Semantics studies meaning in language.
[linguistics] Pragmatics studies how context shapes meaning in communication.
[linguistics] A phoneme is the smallest unit of sound that can distinguish meaning in a language.
[linguistics] A morpheme is the smallest unit of meaning, which may be a whole word or part of one.
[linguistics] Grammar is the set of rules governing how words combine to form sentences in a language.

[linguistics] Chomsky proposed universal grammar, the idea that humans have innate linguistic capacity.
[linguistics] The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis suggests that language shapes thought and perception.
[linguistics] Diachronic linguistics studies how languages change over time.
[linguistics] Synchronic linguistics studies languages at a particular moment without reference to history.
[linguistics] A dialect is a variety of a language associated with a particular region or social group.
[linguistics] An accent refers specifically to pronunciation differences, usually geographic or social.
[linguistics] Pidgins are simplified languages that develop for communication between groups without a common tongue.
[linguistics] Creoles are fully developed languages that evolve from pidgins over generations.
[linguistics] Bilingualism is the ability to use two languages fluently.
[linguistics] Multilingualism refers to fluency in multiple languages.

[linguistics] Content words, like nouns and verbs, carry most of a sentence's meaning.
[linguistics] Function words, like articles and prepositions, structure relationships between content words.
[linguistics] Open word classes, like nouns and verbs, readily accept new members.
[linguistics] Closed word classes, like pronouns and conjunctions, rarely gain new members.
[linguistics] Word order varies across languages: English uses subject-verb-object, Japanese uses subject-object-verb.
[linguistics] Inflection modifies a word to express grammatical features like tense, number, or case.
[linguistics] A lexicon is the vocabulary of a language or a speaker.
[linguistics] Denotation is the literal, dictionary meaning of a word.
[linguistics] Connotation is the emotional or cultural associations a word carries beyond its denotation.
[linguistics] Ambiguity occurs when a word or sentence has more than one possible interpretation.

[cognition] Cognition refers to the mental processes involved in acquiring knowledge and understanding.
[cognition] Attention is the selection of information for deeper processing from a larger stream.
[cognition] Perception is the organization and interpretation of sensory information into experience.
[cognition] Memory involves encoding, storing, and retrieving information across time.
[cognition] Language allows the expression and communication of thoughts through symbols.
[cognition] Reasoning is the process of drawing conclusions from information.
[cognition] Problem-solving involves finding paths from a current state to a desired goal state.
[cognition] Decision-making is choosing among alternatives based on values and information.
[cognition] Metacognition is thinking about one's own thinking, including monitoring and control.
[cognition] Executive function involves planning, focus, working memory, and flexible thinking.

[cognition] Schemas organize knowledge and generate expectations that shape perception and memory.
[cognition] Categories group objects or events by shared features, enabling efficient processing.
[cognition] Prototype theory suggests we represent categories by typical examples.
[cognition] Exemplar theory suggests we represent categories by specific remembered instances.
[cognition] Analogy transfers structure from a familiar domain to understand an unfamiliar one.
[cognition] Abstraction extracts common features across different instances to form general concepts.
[cognition] Conceptual metaphor uses one domain to understand another, like time as a path.
[cognition] Embodied cognition holds that thinking is grounded in bodily experience.
[cognition] Distributed cognition views thinking as extending beyond a single brain across tools and people.
[cognition] Predictive processing views the brain as continuously predicting sensory input and updating based on error.

[cognition] Curiosity is a drive to seek out new information and reduce uncertainty.
[cognition] Intrinsic motivation drives behavior for its own sake, without external reward.
[cognition] Extrinsic motivation drives behavior through external rewards or to avoid punishment.
[cognition] Deliberate practice improves skills through focused, effortful, feedback-rich training.
[cognition] Expertise develops through years of practice and forms rich conceptual structures.
[cognition] The chunking principle groups information into meaningful units to bypass working memory limits.
[cognition] Transfer is applying learning from one context to a different context.
[cognition] Near transfer applies to similar situations, while far transfer applies to distant ones.
[cognition] Creativity often involves combining existing ideas in novel, useful ways.
[cognition] Insight is the sudden understanding of a solution after a period of incubation.

[cognition] Cognitive load is the amount of mental effort demanded by a task.
[cognition] Intrinsic cognitive load comes from the inherent difficulty of the material.
[cognition] Extraneous cognitive load comes from the way information is presented.
[cognition] Germane cognitive load comes from building schemas and understanding.
[cognition] Dual-coding theory suggests we process verbal and visual information in separate but linked systems.
[cognition] The Einstellung effect occurs when prior experience blocks recognition of better solutions.
[cognition] Functional fixedness is the inability to see an object used in unconventional ways.
[cognition] Mental simulation uses imagination to test actions before performing them.
[cognition] Theory of mind is understanding that others have beliefs, desires, and intentions distinct from one's own.
[cognition] Self-awareness is recognizing oneself as an individual distinct from the environment and others.

[communication] Communication is the sharing of information between senders and receivers through shared symbols.
[communication] Verbal communication uses spoken or written words.
[communication] Nonverbal communication includes gesture, facial expression, posture, and tone of voice.
[communication] Active listening involves fully focusing, understanding, and thoughtfully responding to a speaker.
[communication] Clarity in communication comes from precise word choice and organized structure.
[communication] Feedback helps senders adjust their message and confirms receivers' understanding.
[communication] Misunderstanding arises from differences in background, context, or interpretation.
[communication] Rapport is a sense of mutual understanding and trust between people.
[communication] Open questions invite elaboration, while closed questions elicit specific information.
[communication] Effective communicators adapt their style to their audience and context.

[learning] Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior or knowledge due to experience.
[learning] Explicit learning is intentional and conscious, while implicit learning occurs without awareness.
[learning] The zone of proximal development is the range of tasks learnable with guidance but not alone.
[learning] Scaffolding provides temporary support that is gradually removed as competence grows.
[learning] Interleaving mixes different topics during practice and improves long-term retention.
[learning] Spaced repetition reviews material at increasing intervals to strengthen long-term memory.
[learning] Retrieval practice improves memory more than rereading or reviewing.
[learning] Feedback accelerates learning by highlighting errors and confirming successes.
[learning] Self-explanation deepens understanding by requiring learners to articulate their reasoning.
[learning] Metacognitive awareness improves learning by helping learners monitor their own progress.

[knowledge] Knowledge is justified true belief, or information and skills acquired through experience or education.
[knowledge] Declarative knowledge is knowledge of facts that can be stated.
[knowledge] Procedural knowledge is knowledge of how to perform tasks.
[knowledge] Tacit knowledge is hard to articulate, often learned by doing.
[knowledge] Explicit knowledge can be clearly communicated through language or symbols.
[knowledge] Wisdom integrates knowledge with experience, judgment, and ethical insight.
[knowledge] Understanding goes beyond knowing facts to grasp why they are true and how they connect.
[knowledge] Expertise is deep knowledge and skill in a domain, built through extensive practice.
[knowledge] Novice thinkers focus on surface features, while experts see underlying structure.
[knowledge] The Dunning-Kruger effect describes how novices often overestimate their competence.
