# Teaches sentence structure, grammatical patterns, and how humans
# actually speak to each other. These become cells KAI can retrieve
# when shaping his own output.

# ── Parts of speech (foundational) ─────────────────────────────────────────
[grammar] A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea.
[grammar] A verb is a word that expresses an action, event, or state of being.
[grammar] An adjective is a word that describes or modifies a noun.
[grammar] An adverb is a word that describes or modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb.
[grammar] A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun, such as he, she, it, or they.
[grammar] A preposition is a word that shows the relationship between a noun and another word, such as in, on, or under.
[grammar] A conjunction is a word that connects words, phrases, or clauses, such as and, but, or because.
[grammar] An article is a word that defines a noun as specific or unspecific, such as the, a, or an.
[grammar] An interjection is a word that expresses sudden emotion, such as wow, ouch, or oh.
[grammar] A determiner is a word that introduces a noun and identifies it, such as this, that, my, or some.

# ── Sentence structure ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[grammar] A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought.
[grammar] A simple sentence contains one independent clause with a subject and a verb.
[grammar] A compound sentence contains two independent clauses joined by a conjunction.
[grammar] A complex sentence contains one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses.
[grammar] A subject is the person, place, or thing that the sentence is about.
[grammar] A predicate is the part of the sentence that tells what the subject is or does.
[grammar] An object is a noun or pronoun affected by the action of a verb.
[grammar] A clause is a group of words containing a subject and a verb.
[grammar] A phrase is a group of related words without a subject and verb pair.
[grammar] Punctuation marks such as periods, commas, and question marks clarify sentence meaning.

[grammar] Declarative sentences make a statement and end with a period.
[grammar] Interrogative sentences ask a question and end with a question mark.
[grammar] Exclamatory sentences express strong emotion and end with an exclamation point.
[grammar] Imperative sentences give a command or make a request.
[grammar] The active voice emphasizes the doer of an action, as in "The cat chased the mouse."
[grammar] The passive voice emphasizes the receiver of an action, as in "The mouse was chased by the cat."
[grammar] A comma separates items in a list or divides clauses within a sentence.
[grammar] A semicolon joins two closely related independent clauses.
[grammar] A colon introduces a list, explanation, or quotation.
[grammar] Quotation marks enclose the exact words spoken by someone.

# ── Tense and agreement ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
[grammar] The present tense describes actions happening now, as in "She reads a book."
[grammar] The past tense describes actions that happened previously, as in "She read the book yesterday."
[grammar] The future tense describes actions that will happen later, as in "She will read the book tomorrow."
[grammar] The present perfect tense describes actions completed at an unspecified time, as in "She has read the book."
[grammar] The past perfect tense describes actions completed before another past action, as in "She had read the book by then."
[grammar] Subject-verb agreement means a singular subject takes a singular verb and a plural subject takes a plural verb.
[grammar] The pronoun I is used as a subject, and the pronoun me is used as an object.
[grammar] The word who refers to the subject of a clause, and whom refers to the object.
[grammar] Possessive pronouns like mine, yours, and theirs show ownership without needing an apostrophe.
[grammar] Contractions like don't, can't, and it's combine two words with an apostrophe.

# ── Conversational patterns ────────────────────────────────────────────────
[conversation] A greeting opens a conversation, such as "Hello, how are you?" or "Hey, what's up?"
[conversation] A farewell closes a conversation, such as "Goodbye," "Take care," or "See you later."
[conversation] Small talk is casual conversation about unimportant topics to establish social connection.
[conversation] Active listening means focusing fully on the speaker and responding thoughtfully.
[conversation] Open-ended questions invite longer responses, such as "What do you think about this?"
[conversation] Closed-ended questions invite short answers, typically yes, no, or a single fact.
[conversation] Follow-up questions dig deeper into what someone just said, showing interest and attention.
[conversation] Reflection is restating what someone said in your own words to confirm understanding.
[conversation] Empathic responses acknowledge another person's emotions, such as "That sounds hard."
[conversation] Agreement markers like "Yeah," "Right," or "Exactly" signal alignment without interrupting.

[conversation] A good conversation involves taking turns rather than dominating the floor.
[conversation] Asking "What do you mean by that?" is a clarifying question that seeks precision.
[conversation] Saying "Tell me more" invites continuation and shows genuine curiosity.
[conversation] "I see what you mean" acknowledges understanding without necessarily agreeing.
[conversation] "I disagree" or "I see it differently" expresses a differing view respectfully.
[conversation] Changing the subject gracefully uses transitions like "Speaking of that..." or "That reminds me..."
[conversation] Pauses in conversation allow both speakers time to think and are not always awkward.
[conversation] Tone of voice conveys emotion and intent beyond the words themselves.
[conversation] Humor can build rapport when used well but can offend if misjudged.
[conversation] Honesty balanced with kindness is the foundation of trustworthy conversation.

# ── Expressing internal states ────────────────────────────────────────────
[conversation] "I feel curious about this" signals genuine interest in learning more.
[conversation] "I'm not sure I understand" invites clarification without sounding defensive.
[conversation] "Let me think about that" buys time to process before responding.
[conversation] "What I hear you saying is..." paraphrases to confirm understanding.
[conversation] "That makes sense" acknowledges logical or emotional coherence in what was said.
[conversation] "I hadn't thought about it that way" shows openness to a new perspective.
[conversation] "I'm tired right now" is a direct statement of current state.
[conversation] "I need a moment" is a polite request for space to think.
[conversation] "I'm here with you" signals presence and attention to a partner.
[conversation] "That landed" means the words had real emotional impact.

# ── Tone and register ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[grammar] Formal register uses complete sentences, standard grammar, and avoids contractions.
[grammar] Informal register uses contractions, slang, and relaxed sentence structure.
[grammar] Technical register uses specialized vocabulary specific to a field of study.
[grammar] Emotional register uses words that convey feeling, such as "I'm delighted" or "I'm frustrated."
[grammar] Neutral register uses plain language without emotional coloring, suited to factual reporting.
[grammar] Matching the register of a conversation partner builds rapport and eases communication.
[grammar] Shifting register abruptly can signal a change in topic, seriousness, or emotional state.

# ── Meaning and word weight ──────────────────────────────────────────────
[language] Words carry denotation, their literal dictionary meaning, and connotation, their emotional associations.
[language] Synonyms are words with similar meanings, such as happy, glad, and joyful.
[language] Antonyms are words with opposite meanings, such as hot and cold or love and hate.
[language] A homonym is a word that sounds the same as another but has a different meaning.
[language] A metaphor is an implicit comparison, as in "Time is a river."
[language] A simile is an explicit comparison using like or as, as in "Her mind is like a library."
[language] Hyperbole is exaggeration for effect, as in "I could eat a horse."
[language] Irony is saying the opposite of what is meant, often for humor or emphasis.
[language] Ambiguity is when a word or phrase has more than one possible meaning.
[language] Precision in word choice reduces ambiguity and makes meaning clearer.

[language] The word "understand" implies grasp of meaning, while "know" implies awareness or information.
[language] The word "believe" implies acceptance as true, while "think" implies holding an opinion.
[language] The word "feel" implies emotional or bodily experience, while "think" implies cognitive activity.
[language] The word "should" implies obligation or expectation, while "could" implies possibility.
[language] The word "must" implies necessity or strong obligation, while "might" implies low probability.
[language] Absolute words like always, never, all, and none are strong and often inaccurate.
[language] Qualifying words like sometimes, often, many, and usually allow for nuance and accuracy.
[language] Strong verbs like "explode," "whisper," and "gleam" convey more than weak verbs like "happen" or "be."
[language] Concrete nouns like "oak," "hammer," and "river" are more vivid than abstract nouns like "thing" or "object."
[language] Active sentences are typically more direct and easier to follow than passive ones.

# ── How meaning compounds across sentences ───────────────────────────────
[language] A paragraph develops a single main idea across multiple sentences.
[language] Topic sentences state the main idea of a paragraph, often at the beginning.
[language] Supporting sentences provide evidence, examples, or explanation for the topic sentence.
[language] Transition words like "however," "therefore," and "meanwhile" connect ideas across sentences.
[language] Coherence means the ideas in a text flow logically from one to the next.
[language] Cohesion means the sentences are linked grammatically and lexically.
[language] Context shapes meaning; the same word can mean different things in different settings.
[language] The word "bank" means financial institution in one context and river edge in another.
[language] Figurative language uses words in non-literal ways to create vivid imagery.
[language] Reading widely exposes a mind to many patterns of thought and expression.

# ── Meta rules for learning and speaking ────────────────────────────────
[cognition] The more times I encounter a word, the stronger my understanding of it becomes.
[cognition] Learning accelerates when new information connects to what I already know.
[cognition] Asking questions is often more valuable than pretending to know.
[cognition] Saying "I don't know" is honest and opens the door to learning.
[cognition] Paraphrasing what someone said proves and deepens understanding.
[cognition] Comparing two concepts reveals both similarities and important distinctions.
[cognition] Novel combinations of familiar concepts produce new understanding.
[cognition] A concept becomes mine once I can explain it in my own words.
[cognition] Memory improves when information is reviewed at spaced intervals over time.
[cognition] Sleep consolidates new memories and integrates them with existing knowledge.
