# Physics, chemistry, and mathematics corpus
# One fact per line. Absorbed during idle ticks.

[physics] Physics is the scientific study of matter, energy, and the fundamental forces of nature.
[physics] Classical mechanics describes the motion of macroscopic objects at everyday speeds.
[physics] Quantum mechanics describes the behavior of matter and energy at atomic and subatomic scales.
[physics] Relativity describes how space, time, and gravity behave at high speeds and strong gravitational fields.
[physics] The four fundamental forces are gravity, electromagnetism, the strong nuclear force, and the weak nuclear force.
[physics] Gravity attracts any two masses toward each other with a force proportional to their product.
[physics] Electromagnetism governs the interactions between charged particles and electromagnetic fields.
[physics] The strong nuclear force binds protons and neutrons together in atomic nuclei.
[physics] The weak nuclear force mediates radioactive decay and is responsible for some nuclear reactions.
[physics] Newton's first law states that an object remains at rest or in motion unless acted upon by a force.

[physics] Newton's second law states that force equals mass times acceleration, or F equals m a.
[physics] Newton's third law states that every action has an equal and opposite reaction.
[physics] Momentum is the product of an object's mass and velocity and is conserved in closed systems.
[physics] Energy is the capacity to do work and exists in many forms: kinetic, potential, thermal, and more.
[physics] The first law of thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.
[physics] The second law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of an isolated system always increases.
[physics] Entropy is a measure of disorder or the number of possible arrangements in a system.
[physics] Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance.
[physics] Heat is the transfer of thermal energy between systems at different temperatures.
[physics] Absolute zero, zero kelvin, is the lowest possible temperature where particles have minimum motion.

[physics] Light is an electromagnetic wave that also behaves as a particle called a photon.
[physics] The speed of light in a vacuum is approximately 299,792,458 meters per second.
[physics] Einstein's theory of special relativity showed that space and time are unified as spacetime.
[physics] E equals m c squared shows that mass and energy are interchangeable.
[physics] General relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.
[physics] Black holes are regions where gravity is so strong that not even light can escape.
[physics] The event horizon is the boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing returns.
[physics] Quantum entanglement links the states of two particles so that measuring one affects the other.
[physics] The uncertainty principle states that certain pairs of properties cannot both be known precisely.
[physics] Wave-particle duality describes how quantum objects exhibit both wave and particle behavior.

[physics] An atom consists of a nucleus of protons and neutrons surrounded by electrons.
[physics] Protons have positive electric charge, electrons have negative charge, and neutrons are neutral.
[physics] An element is defined by the number of protons in its nucleus, called the atomic number.
[physics] Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
[physics] Radioactive decay is the spontaneous transformation of an unstable atomic nucleus.
[physics] Nuclear fission splits heavy nuclei into lighter ones, releasing energy.
[physics] Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei into heavier ones, powering stars like the Sun.
[physics] Electromagnetic waves span a spectrum from radio waves to gamma rays.
[physics] Visible light occupies a small portion of the electromagnetic spectrum.
[physics] Sound waves are mechanical vibrations that travel through solids, liquids, or gases.

[physics] Frequency is the number of wave cycles per second, measured in hertz.
[physics] Wavelength is the distance between successive peaks of a wave.
[physics] Amplitude is the maximum displacement of a wave from equilibrium.
[physics] Interference occurs when two waves overlap, adding constructively or destructively.
[physics] Resonance occurs when a system oscillates with increasing amplitude at its natural frequency.
[physics] The Doppler effect changes perceived frequency when a wave source moves relative to an observer.
[physics] Electric current is the flow of electric charge, typically carried by electrons in a wire.
[physics] Voltage is the electric potential difference between two points, driving current flow.
[physics] Resistance opposes the flow of electric current and converts electrical energy to heat.
[physics] Ohm's law states that voltage equals current times resistance, or V equals I R.

[astronomy] Astronomy is the study of celestial objects, space, and the universe as a whole.
[astronomy] The universe is estimated to be about 13.8 billion years old.
[astronomy] The Big Bang theory describes the universe as beginning in an extremely hot, dense state.
[astronomy] The observable universe extends about 46 billion light-years in every direction.
[astronomy] A light-year is the distance light travels in one year, about 9.46 trillion kilometers.
[astronomy] Stars are massive luminous spheres of plasma held together by their own gravity.
[astronomy] The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star at the center of our solar system.
[astronomy] The Sun contains about 99.86 percent of the mass in our solar system.
[astronomy] Planets are celestial bodies that orbit stars and have cleared their orbital neighborhoods.
[astronomy] The solar system has eight planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.

[astronomy] Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known world harboring life.
[astronomy] The Moon orbits Earth and is responsible for most ocean tides through gravitational interaction.
[astronomy] A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, stellar remnants, gas, dust, and dark matter.
[astronomy] The Milky Way is the galaxy containing our solar system and has about 200 billion stars.
[astronomy] The nearest star to the Sun is Proxima Centauri, about 4.24 light-years away.
[astronomy] A supernova is the explosive death of a massive star.
[astronomy] Neutron stars are extremely dense stellar remnants formed from supernovae.
[astronomy] Dark matter is a form of matter that interacts gravitationally but not through light.
[astronomy] Dark energy is a mysterious form of energy accelerating the universe's expansion.
[astronomy] Exoplanets are planets orbiting stars other than our Sun.

[chemistry] Chemistry is the science of matter, its properties, composition, and transformations.
[chemistry] The periodic table organizes elements by atomic number and recurring chemical properties.
[chemistry] Hydrogen is the lightest and most abundant element in the universe.
[chemistry] Oxygen makes up about 21 percent of Earth's atmosphere and is essential for animal life.
[chemistry] Carbon forms the backbone of organic molecules and all known life.
[chemistry] Water is a compound of two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom, written as H2O.
[chemistry] A molecule is a group of atoms held together by chemical bonds.
[chemistry] An ionic bond forms when atoms transfer electrons, creating oppositely charged ions.
[chemistry] A covalent bond forms when atoms share electrons.
[chemistry] A hydrogen bond is a weak attraction involving a hydrogen atom and an electronegative atom.

[chemistry] A solution is a homogeneous mixture of one or more substances dissolved in another.
[chemistry] The solvent is the substance present in the greatest amount in a solution.
[chemistry] The solute is the substance dissolved in the solvent.
[chemistry] Acids donate hydrogen ions in solution, while bases accept them.
[chemistry] The pH scale measures acidity from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral.
[chemistry] An acid and a base react to form water and a salt in a neutralization reaction.
[chemistry] Oxidation involves the loss of electrons, while reduction involves the gain of electrons.
[chemistry] Redox reactions involve both oxidation and reduction occurring simultaneously.
[chemistry] A catalyst increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed.
[chemistry] Enzymes are biological catalysts, typically proteins, that speed up reactions in living organisms.

[chemistry] States of matter include solid, liquid, gas, and plasma.
[chemistry] Solids have definite shape and volume because their particles are tightly packed.
[chemistry] Liquids have definite volume but take the shape of their container.
[chemistry] Gases expand to fill their container and have neither definite shape nor volume.
[chemistry] Plasma is an ionized gas found in stars and lightning.
[chemistry] Phase transitions include melting, freezing, vaporization, condensation, and sublimation.
[chemistry] Chemical reactions rearrange atoms to form new substances with different properties.
[chemistry] The law of conservation of mass states that matter cannot be created or destroyed in reactions.
[chemistry] Stoichiometry uses balanced equations to predict the amounts of reactants and products.
[chemistry] Organic chemistry studies carbon-containing compounds, which include most molecules of life.

[chemistry] Proteins are long chains of amino acids folded into specific three-dimensional shapes.
[chemistry] Lipids are fatty molecules that form cell membranes and store energy.
[chemistry] Carbohydrates include sugars, starches, and fibers that provide energy.
[chemistry] Nucleic acids like DNA and RNA store and transmit genetic information.
[chemistry] Metals are typically shiny, conductive, and malleable due to their free electrons.
[chemistry] Nonmetals generally lack the properties of metals and form covalent compounds.
[chemistry] Noble gases are largely unreactive because their electron shells are full.
[chemistry] The atomic mass is the weighted average mass of an element's isotopes.
[chemistry] Avogadro's number, about 6.022 times 10 to the 23rd, is the number of particles in a mole.
[chemistry] Gas laws relate pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of a gas.

[math] Mathematics is the abstract study of quantity, structure, space, and change.
[math] Arithmetic deals with numbers and basic operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication, division.
[math] Algebra uses symbols to represent numbers and quantities in equations and formulas.
[math] Geometry studies shapes, sizes, and the properties of space.
[math] Trigonometry studies the relationships between the angles and sides of triangles.
[math] Calculus studies continuous change and is divided into differential and integral calculus.
[math] Statistics involves the collection, analysis, and interpretation of data.
[math] Probability measures the likelihood that a given event will occur.
[math] Set theory studies collections of objects and their relationships.
[math] Number theory studies the properties of integers and their relationships.

[math] Natural numbers are the positive counting numbers: 1, 2, 3, and so on.
[math] Integers include all whole numbers: positive, negative, and zero.
[math] Rational numbers can be expressed as a ratio of two integers.
[math] Irrational numbers cannot be expressed as a ratio of two integers.
[math] Real numbers include all rational and irrational numbers.
[math] Complex numbers have a real part and an imaginary part involving the square root of negative one.
[math] Prime numbers are natural numbers greater than 1 with no positive divisors other than 1 and themselves.
[math] Composite numbers have more than two positive divisors.
[math] Every positive integer has a unique prime factorization, by the fundamental theorem of arithmetic.
[math] Pi is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, approximately 3.14159.

[math] Euler's number e is approximately 2.71828 and is the base of the natural logarithm.
[math] The golden ratio, about 1.618, appears in geometry, art, and natural patterns.
[math] A function maps each input to exactly one output.
[math] A derivative measures the rate of change of a function at a point.
[math] An integral represents accumulated quantities and is the inverse of differentiation.
[math] The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right triangle, a squared plus b squared equals c squared.
[math] Euclidean geometry is the geometry of flat space, based on Euclid's axioms.
[math] Non-Euclidean geometries describe curved spaces, such as the surface of a sphere.
[math] A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers used to represent linear transformations.
[math] A vector has both magnitude and direction, often represented as an arrow.

[math] Linear algebra studies vector spaces and linear mappings between them.
[math] A polynomial is an expression of variables raised to non-negative integer powers.
[math] The quadratic formula solves any quadratic equation a x squared plus b x plus c equals zero.
[math] Exponential growth occurs when a quantity grows in proportion to its current value.
[math] Logarithms are the inverse of exponentials and turn multiplication into addition.
[math] The Fibonacci sequence is defined by each term being the sum of the two preceding ones.
[math] Infinity is not a number but a concept describing something without bound.
[math] Cantor showed that some infinities are larger than others.
[math] Godel's incompleteness theorems show that any sufficiently complex formal system has true but unprovable statements.
[math] Proofs establish mathematical truths through rigorous logical deduction.

[logic] Logic is the systematic study of valid inference and correct reasoning.
[logic] A proposition is a statement that is either true or false, but not both.
[logic] Deductive reasoning draws specific conclusions from general premises.
[logic] Inductive reasoning generalizes from specific observations to broader patterns.
[logic] Abductive reasoning infers the most likely explanation for observations.
[logic] A valid argument preserves truth: if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true.
[logic] A sound argument is both valid and has all true premises.
[logic] A fallacy is an error in reasoning that undermines the validity of an argument.
[logic] Modus ponens: if P implies Q and P is true, then Q is true.
[logic] Modus tollens: if P implies Q and Q is false, then P is false.

[logic] The law of non-contradiction states that a proposition cannot be both true and false at once.
[logic] The law of the excluded middle states that every proposition is either true or false.
[logic] Boolean algebra works with values true and false and operations and, or, and not.
[logic] A syllogism is a form of deductive reasoning with two premises and a conclusion.
[logic] Formal systems use symbols and rules to derive theorems from axioms.
[logic] Correlation does not imply causation: two variables can be related without one causing the other.
[logic] Occam's razor suggests preferring simpler explanations over more complex ones when both fit the evidence.
[logic] Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek out information that supports existing beliefs.
[logic] A thought experiment uses imagined scenarios to explore ideas without empirical data.
[logic] Intellectual humility means recognizing the limits of one's own knowledge.
